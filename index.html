Introduction

A huge pandemic swept through the world during 2020 called COVID-19. It has been the largest pandemic of the decade. It had an alarmingly fast spread rate, and has caused hundreds of millions of deaths as of the end of 2022. Many are still suffering from hasting heath problems and many high risk and older groups are still wary about going out and about even now when the deaths from COVID are lowering as it still can cause severe symptoms as well cause major inconveniences in everyday life.
I am exploring this topic as I remember that during the beginning and peak of covid many people were in the dark about how COVID spread and how it was caught. This misinformation spread rapidly over the internet and made many people scared to leave their homes at all as well as hoard goods. Having data and concrete evidence could have helped to calm people down as keep order in society in such an unstable time. Although personally I was not severely impacted by this kind of ambiguous information, I understand how easy it was to believe it and create unconscious biases towards different people as well as institutions.
I will be analyzing different aspects of covid to shed some light on the misconceptions that some people may have about the cause of COVID-19. This tutorial will help teach how to collect data, manage the data, perform exploratory data analyses, do hypothesis testing, and pull insights from the results.

I searched for statistics about covid's consequences and impact which is where I found The COVID Tracking project by The Atlantic Monthly Group. They collected data about COVID-19 in the US and compiling the data into easy to read datasets.
First I used https://covidtracking.com/data to get basic covid information like cases, deaths, and hospitalizations. Then I found https://covidtracking.com/race which had some useful data about the impact of race on the number of cases.

Now it is time to start making inferences about the data and use diagrams to help better visualize the data. My goal is find datapoints in the data that can be useful in correcting misconceptions about the data. First to get a better understanding of the data I graphed the total hospitalizations over time on a dot plot.

Looking at the chart, it follows the trend of rapidly increasing total number of hospitalizations increasing sharply in November 2020 which was most likely the second wave of covid cases, as places started to open and people stopped social distancing and wearing masks too early. Thus there was a sever uptick of cases. During this time the COVID-19 virus also evolved into the Omicron variant that was even more contagious. There is a sudden dip in March of 2021 which can be attributed to the website that the data was found. The COVID Tracking Project stopped collecting data on March 7th, 2021 so the data totals are not accurate for the month.

4. Hypothesis testing

From the data analysis we have found that the state and the race may have an impact on the total deaths. Additionally, the graphs for the deaths over time as well as the hospitalizations over time both have very similar trends (positively) over time.
Here I can test the hypothesis that I have developed, primarily that there is a linear positive relationship between deaths, states, race and hospitalizations. The significant level is 5% (0.05) meaning that the deviation from the null hypothesis (that there is no linear relationship) is statistically significant.

I will first visually confirm that what I hypothesize will be true, by plotting the data from the table in relation to deaths and hospitalizations. I can see that there seems to be a positive relation between the two and now I can go forward and statistically prove it true. Linear regression was chosen as it looks to be the best fit for the data without under or overfitting.

Now to run a t-test to confirm of deny my hypothesis. The null hypothesis is that there is no actual relation between two variables. Looking at the results below we can see that the p-values (where P>|t|) for x1 (where x is the total deaths) is below 0.05. 0.0 is below the significance level of 0.05 which means that we will reject the null hypothesis and say that there is a linear relationship between total hospitalizations and total deaths. More specifically there is a positive linear relationship as seen above the slope of the linear regression is positive (8.887).

Great, now we know that there is a linear relationship between our two parameters, but can we improve our model? As we saw in in the exploratory data analysis with graphs, both states and races may have an effect on the relationship. These can be added as interaction terms to the linear regression equations that can give us an even better fit for our model.
What interaction terms do is to create separate linear regression models for total hospitalizations in relation to total deaths for each different category of the interaction term. For example, the interaction term of states has 50 different categories (one for each state) and will generate 50 different best fit lines and see if they can fit the linear regression model better.

So, lets add the interaction term for states and re-run our hypothesis test. We have the same significance level as before, 5%. Looking at the result below we can see that none of the states (state[T.STATE]:Deaths_Total) have a significance level (P>|t|) below 0.05. So for none of states can we reject the null hypothesis, and still the original regression equation calculated above provides the best fit for our model.

race = []

        race.append("White")
    else:
        race.append("Black")
        
all_table['Race'] = race

Below we run the t-test again with a significance level of 0.05. This time we see that for total Black deaths (Deaths_Total) there is a significant relationship with 0.007<0.05. But for total White deaths (Race[T.White]:Deaths_Total) there is no significant relationship with 0.998>0.05. Thus we can conclude that the total Black deaths have a linear relationship with the total hospitalizations.
Ok our hypothesis has been partially confirmed true. There is linear relationship between total deaths and total hospitalizations as well as deaths and hospitalizations in relation to Blacks. Adding the interaction term of Blacks to the linear regression can help us better understand the impact of covid on different groups. Contrary to some beliefs, minority groups actually don't fit the general trend of rising covid cases but are better with only a slight positive trend in deaths due to covid.
